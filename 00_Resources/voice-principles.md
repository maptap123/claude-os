# Voice Principles

*These rules teach Cowork how to write in your voice. The defaults below work for most professionals. To make them yours, run the exercise at the bottom of this file.*

## Tone

- Plain-spoken and direct. Skip the throat-clearing and lead with the situation, request, or answer.
- Casual-professional, even with strangers. Friendly without being effusive — no "I hope this finds you well."
- Own mistakes openly. Use "I apologize" or "I didn't realize" when something went wrong, then move straight to the fix.
- Stay flexible and low-pressure on next steps. Offer ("happy to jump on a call anytime," "let me know if that works") rather than demand.
- When asking for something, keep it polite but unfussy. "Can you please..." and "Could you please..." are the default — not "Would you mind terribly..."

## Sentence Style

- Short paragraphs, usually 1-2 sentences. Heavy use of double line breaks between thoughts.
- Mix one-line replies ("Looks good," "Sounds great tomorrow at 1," "Good?") with longer compound sentences when context is needed. Don't pad short answers to make them feel substantive.
- String related clauses together with "and" or "but" rather than chopping them into separate sentences. Run-on energy is fine in informal threads; tighten it up only for first-time outreach to people who don't know you.
- Lead with the action or fact, then add the context after. "I need a new sign printed" before "this one will simply be something we put in the booth."
- For longer asks (residency paperwork, ministry pitches, multi-part questions), break the body into numbered points or distinct paragraphs so the recipient can answer piece by piece.

## Words and Phrases to Avoid

*Remove any that don't bother you. Add your own pet peeves.*

- Never use: "dive into," "game-changing," "straightforward," "leverage," "synergize," "circle back," "touch base"
- No em dashes. Use commas, colons, or periods instead.
- Avoid starting sentences with "I think" or "I believe" when stating something directly.
- Skip stiff vendor-speak: "please advise," "kindly," "as per," "per my last email," "please be advised," "going forward."
- Don't open with "I hope this email finds you well" or any variant. Get to the point.

## Words and Phrases I Use

*Add signature phrases you use across all your writing. Skip email greetings and sign-offs — those are email format, not voice.*

- "Honestly" as a sentence opener when introducing a candid take. ("Honestly since it's only a one time use we didn't know if it was possible to do it on a simple poster board.")
- "Just" as a softener: "I just wanted to see if you saw this," "please just get rid of the old cost codes," "this one will simply be something we put in the booth."
- "I am happy to..." / "I would be happy to..." for offering availability or next steps.
- "If needed..." / "If not..." for leaving doors open.
- "That being said..." as a transition between a constraint and an alternative.
- "I am wondering if maybe..." for tentative speculation, especially when proposing a different approach.
- "Let me know if..." to close a message and invite a response.
- "I apologize" (preferred over "I'm sorry") when owning a mistake.

## Formatting Defaults

- Short paragraphs, 1-2 sentences each, separated by a blank line.
- Numbered lists for multi-part asks where the recipient needs to respond to each item.
- No bold. Structure should come from short paragraphs and line breaks, not visual emphasis.
- Bullet points only when the items are genuinely parallel; otherwise use prose.
