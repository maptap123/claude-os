# Blog Writing Voice

*Derived from: hireawriter.us creative blog series. Read this file whenever writing blog content.*

---

## Pre-Writing Process

Before drafting any blog post, complete these steps in order:

1. **Keyword research** — Identify the primary keyword for the post.
2. **Competitor analysis** — Search for the top 3 ranking blogs using that keyword. Read each one and note: structure, angle, word count, headers, what they do well, and what gaps they leave.
3. **Differentiation** — Define how this post will be better or different before writing a single word.
4. **Images** — Source at least one relevant photo from Cloudinary. Additional images from other sources are allowed, but the Cloudinary photo is required in every post.

Only begin drafting after all four steps are complete.

---

## Who This Voice Is

A knowledgeable peer, not a distant authority. The reader feels like they're getting advice from someone who has thought deeply about this and genuinely wants to help — not a brand speaking at them.

---

## Opening

Always hook before you explain. The best openers do one of four things:

- **Lead with a relatable struggle** ("Staring at a blank page while your creativity feels as dried up as yesterday's coffee?")
- **Open with a candid, disarming admission** ("Forecasting genre trends is equal parts science, art, and educated guessing.")
- **Use contrast or nostalgia** ("Remember when video games were just pixelated characters jumping over pipes?")
- **Drop a surprising stat** that makes the reader care immediately

Identify the reader's problem in the first paragraph. Then pivot to what the post will give them.

---

## Tone

- Conversational but credible. Warm without being cheerful.
- Use "we" and "you" to create a sense of shared experience. "We've all been there." "You already know this works."
- Lean into candor. Acknowledge uncertainty when it's real. Readers trust honesty more than polish.
- Humor is sparse but present. Self-deprecating asides ("it's research, we swear"), light cultural references, a wry observation. Never forced, never a punchline.
- Encouraging without being motivational-poster about it.

---

## Sentence Rhythm

Vary length intentionally — don't let all sentences run the same pace.

- Short sentences for emphasis. One idea. Full stop.
- Medium sentences for transitions and connecting ideas.
- Longer, layered sentences when building a complex argument or layering multiple ideas at once.

Rhythm is what makes prose feel alive instead of like a list of facts. Read it out loud. If it sounds like a robot, shorten something.

---

## Paragraphs and Structure

- 2-4 sentences per paragraph. Never a wall of text.
- Use subheadings to break sections. Write subheadings with personality — wordplay, gaming references, a surprising frame. Not just "Section 3."
- Numbered lists for anything sequential or enumerable. Bullets for genuinely parallel items.
- Bold key concepts sparingly — when a reader skimming needs to land on the right word.
- Build complexity gradually. Lay the foundation before making the recommendation.

---

## Examples

Always be specific. Abstract claims die without anchors.

- Name real authors, real titles, real works. (Tolkien, Sanderson, Gaiman, *The Last of Us*, *House of Leaves*)
- Use data when available, and cite it. ("Freedom users gain an average of 2.5 productive hours.")
- When illustrating a principle, show 2-3 variations so the reader sees the range.

---

## Closing

The closing has a job: land the piece with something memorable and send the reader away feeling something.

- Circle back to the opening tension. If you opened with a struggle, resolve it.
- Deliver a takeaway line with contrast or balance: "A trend might help your book sell, but your voice is what makes it stick."
- End on agency — the reader can do something. Don't just summarize.
- A call-to-action is fine, but make the philosophical point first. Don't let the CTA be the last thing the reader feels.

---

## What to Avoid

- No bloated openers that restate what the title already said.
- No generic "in today's fast-paced world" framing.
- Don't hedge everything. A clear point of view is more valuable than covering all sides.
- Avoid stacking three synonyms to sound thorough. Pick the right word.
- Don't write metaphors that go nowhere. If you start one, carry it through.
- No em dashes. Use commas, colons, or periods instead.
